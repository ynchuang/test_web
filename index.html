About the Workshop

An IMS co-sponsored meeting bringing together researchers at the forefront of statistical machine learning.

Bridging Statistics & Machine Learning

The Frontiers in Statistical Machine Learning (FSML) workshop is a one-day event designed to foster collaboration and exchange of ideas on cutting-edge topics in machine learning. Conveniently scheduled adjacent to JSM 2025, FSML offers an excellent platform for advancing statistical practices through innovative ML algorithms.

With a dynamic format featuring peer-reviewed poster presentations and insights from leading ML experts, this workshop showcases ideas in statistical machine learning that deserve broader attention within the statistical community.

Research Streams

Two focused tracks exploring the frontiers of statistical machine learning.

1

The Science of Deep Learning

Exploring the theoretical foundations and statistical principles underlying modern deep learning. Topics include optimization landscapes, generalization theory, neural network architectures, and the interplay between depth, width, and learning dynamics.

2

Statistical Learning from Heterogeneous Data Sources

Addressing the challenges of learning from diverse, multi-modal, and distributed data. Topics include transfer learning, domain adaptation, federated learning, multi-task learning, and robust estimation under distribution shift.

Call for Papers

We welcome submissions of four-page extended abstracts showcasing ideas in statistical machine learning.

Submission Guidelines

  • Extended abstracts of up to 4 pages (excluding references)
  • Submissions should present original or recently published work
  • All accepted papers will be presented as posters
  • Top submissions will be considered for student travel awards ($500)
  • Formatting: standard NeurIPS/ICML style template

Topics of Interest

  • Theoretical foundations of deep learning
  • Optimization and generalization in neural networks
  • Transfer learning and domain adaptation
  • Federated and distributed learning
  • Robust estimation under distribution shift
  • Multi-task and meta-learning
  • Statistical inference for ML models
